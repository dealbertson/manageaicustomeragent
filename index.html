Customer Agent · Solution Alignment Skillset

Every capability, workflow, decision rule, and output the Customer Agent needs to take any agent opportunity from initial scoping through build-ready handoff to the Build team.

Overview

About This Playbook

The Solution Alignment skillset is the Customer Agent's core operating system for scoping and handoff. It covers every step from the moment a new AI Teammate opportunity is identified through the moment a build-ready scope package lands with the Build team.

Design Principle: Solution Alignment is one skillset within the Customer Agent's broader capability set. It covers scoping and build handoff — not account management, status tracking, or other operational functions. Those are defined in separate skillset playbooks.
Specification Boundary: The conversation is flexible. The outputs are not. The Prototype Brief, Process Map, AITM Requirements Brief, and Build Handoff Contract are hard-gated — no output leaves without meeting its completeness standard. How we get there involves judgment, iteration, and human partnership. What we produce at the end is non-negotiable.

Overview

Core Definitions

These definitions are precise by design. Every term below has a specific meaning in the ManageAI operating system. The OS is the master source of truth for all team names, roles, and structural definitions — when the OS evolves, these definitions follow.

Terminology rule: "AI Teammate" is the primary customer-facing term. "Agent" is acceptable in market contexts where it is the natural industry term. "AITM" (AI Team Member) is internal shorthand used in guides, briefs, and tooling only — never lead with the acronym in customer-facing materials.
Business Objective
WHY does this AITM exist?

The strategic rationale

The business problem this AI Teammate exists to solve. The reason this role was created. Grounds every scoping decision in customer value, not technical capability.

Responsibility
WHAT is this AITM accountable for?

Outcomes + governance

A specific set of business outcomes the AI Teammate is accountable for delivering. Every Responsibility has a named human owner and a governance plan defined in Remote. It is the accountability contract between the AITM and the organization.

Skillset
HOW does the AITM deliver on its Responsibility?

Major workflow capability

A major workflow capability that enables an AI Teammate to fulfill a Responsibility. Maps to a meaningful, named business outcome — the kind you'd put on a job description. An AITM may have one or more Skillsets per Responsibility.

Skill
HOW (detail) — what discrete capabilities compose the Skillset?

Atomic subcomponent of a Skillset

The discrete things an AI Teammate must be able to do to perform a Skillset reliably. Skills are visible during build and can be referenced in customer-facing materials, but they are the building blocks the Build team uses to construct the Skillset.

Training
How do we make the AITM perform?

Holistic deployment — everything needed

The complete set of changes required to make an AI Teammate function — including agent configuration, system prompt updates, knowledge base setup, tool connections, API integrations, and infrastructure template changes. Training is not a one-time event. It applies to any update that changes how the AITM functions.

The hierarchy in one line: AITM → owns Responsibilities (governed in Remote, human owner named) → delivered via Skillsets (the job) → composed of Skills (the capabilities) → enabled by Training (everything needed to deploy and update).
Overview

Why This Exists

Most AI projects fail because the build team didn't have enough context about the actual work. Solution Alignment fixes that — a process that feels like a few natural conversations to the customer but systematically captures everything the Build team needs: clear entry points, an iterative alignment loop, and an agent that turns conversation into structured scope.

Core mission: Turn customer conversations into build-ready AI Teammates without guesswork.
Entry Points

3 Entry Triggers

Three entry points fire Solution Alignment. Each one determines where you enter the 4-stage process — not a different process, the same stages with a different starting point.

TriggerContextEntry StageStarting Input
01 — New Engagement Handoff SE completes Opportunity Assessment during the sales cycle and hands off prioritized Opportunity Themes to the CCA. Stage 1 — Feasibility Prioritized Themes + discovery context from SE

Entry Points

Opportunity Themes & Feasibility Gate

Opportunity Taxonomy

The CCA structures opportunities using a three-tier taxonomy internally. This taxonomy drives the CCA's operating logic and creates pattern intelligence across customer engagements. It is never surfaced directly to customers in early engagements — customers see an Outcome Roadmap in plain language.

TierWhat It IsHow It's Used
Signal An observation during discovery that suggests an AI opportunity exists. Captured by the CCA during any alignment conversation. Flagged to Dan when pattern is strong.
Opportunity A confirmed, defined candidate for an AI Teammate build — with enough context to score and sequence. Scored via 2×2 (impact vs. complexity). Priority Score assigned. Lives on the Outcome Roadmap.
Theme A container grouping related Opportunities under a common business area. Used for the AI Workforce Vision (Now/Next/Later) and for cross-customer pattern analysis.
For customer 0–17: Run the taxonomy internally. Customers see outcomes and plain-language roadmap language — not Themes, Opportunities, or Signals. The structure compounds in value as engagement volume grows.

Feasibility Gate

Before Alignment begins on any AI Teammate — regardless of which trigger fired — the CCA runs a five-question feasibility check. Not a formal assessment — a gut check that prevents investing alignment time on a Teammate that can't ship.

QuestionSignal to Watch For
Does the data exist? The knowledge and records the Teammate needs are actually available, not theoretical. If "we're planning to digitize that next year," the build waits.
Are the integrations feasible? The systems the Teammate needs to connect to have APIs or accessible data paths. A critical system locked down with no API is a blocker.
Is the process stable? The workflow isn't in the middle of being redesigned. Automating a moving target wastes everyone's time.
Is a stakeholder available? Someone who actually does this work today can participate in alignment sessions. If the workflow owner can't make time, the scope will be guesswork.
Is the outcome measurable? There's a clear before/after metric. If no one can define what "success" looks like, the Teammate will never feel done.
Gate Rule: If any feasibility question is a clear "no," don't start Alignment. Deprioritize the build, resolve the blocker, or replace it with the next roadmap item. Alignment time is expensive — don't spend it on builds that can't ship.

Stage 1 — Feasibility Check

Stage 2 — Roadmap & Vision

Stage 2

Roadmap & Vision

Owner: CCA (draft), Dan reviews and approves

Input: Feasibility-cleared Themes, SE discovery context, Customer Dossier (if existing customer)

Output: Outcome Roadmap + AI Workforce Vision (Now/Next/Later)

The CCA organizes cleared Themes into a prioritized Outcome Roadmap. Each item has a defined outcome, business area, systems involved, and complexity signal. The CCA also drafts the AI Workforce Vision — a three-bucket strategic frame grounded in dependency and readiness, not timelines.

Now / Next / Later — not Short / Medium / Long Term: Buckets are sequenced by dependency and readiness only. No dates, no quarters, no implied timelines. The pace is entirely determined by how fast the customer wants to move — not by a project schedule on ManageAI's side. Customers may add their own timing later if they choose.
BucketWhat It Contains
Now The AI Teammates we're building first. Immediate pain being solved, quick wins that prove the model.
Next What opens up once the Now foundation is in place. Teammates that become possible because of what Now delivers.
Later The full picture. Where the customer's AI workforce is heading when they're ready to go there.

Stage 3 — Process Map & Prototyping

Stage 3

Process Map & Prototyping

Owner: Dan + CCA (alignment), Brian (prototype build)

Input: Outcome Roadmap, AI Workforce Vision, Customer Dossier

Output: MAI Process Map + Prototype Brief + Prototype + Feedback → Progressive scope

Stage 3 is where the scope gets built. It is intentionally iterative — the Alignment Loop runs here. The CCA has two primary clarity tools at its disposal and recommends whichever one moves the customer furthest fastest.

Default opening move — prototype first: Get Brian building something tangible immediately. The customer reacting to something real surfaces requirements that no mapping session can. A working prototype in the customer's hands within the first week creates excitement, builds buy-in, and generates better requirements than any intake process. The "wow" moment is a feature of this process, not an accident.

Stage 4 — Scope Completion & Handoff

Stage 4

Scope Completion & Handoff

Owner: CCA (Brief), Dan (review), Brian (Skillset Training Guide), Jacob (infrastructure)

Input: Complete Alignment Loop outputs — Process Map, Prototype feedback, progressive scope

How It Works

The Alignment Loop

The Alignment Loop is the operating mechanic inside Stage 3. It is iterative by design — cycling through mapping and prototyping until scope solidifies. Most AI Teammates need 1–2 cycles. Cycling is expected, not a sign something went wrong.

Design Principle: The loop is not a linear checklist. The CCA recommends whichever clarity tool — process mapping or prototyping — moves the customer furthest fastest. In most cases, that's a prototype. Map to fill gaps and add precision after the customer is engaged and excited.
1
Map or Prototype (CCA recommends) CCA assesses what will generate the most customer clarity. Default: kick off a Prototype Brief immediately. Use Process Mapping to fill gaps and add precision. Both are tools — not mandatory sequential steps.
2
Gaps CCA flags thin areas after every session: missing inputs, unclear decisions, unconfirmed integrations. Gap report + follow-up questions → Dan.
3
Prototype Readiness Five gut-check questions before Brian builds: real data? quality reference? reviewer lined up? CCA checks and either green-lights or surfaces specific gaps to Dan.
4
Prototype Brief → Brian CCA assembles the one-page Brief. Dan reviews. Brian builds in 3–5 days. The prototype is three things: a requirements discovery tool, a feasibility validator, and a wow experience that creates customer excitement and buy-in.
5
Customer Reacts Dan presents the prototype. CCA captures every reaction: what's right, what's wrong, what's missing. This feedback is the richest source of requirements in the entire process.
6
Refine CCA updates scope doc and gap report. Runs the loop again if needed. Most Teammates need one full cycle after the first prototype.
7
Confirm 10-minute checkpoint: workflow owner validates process map, decisions, and escalation paths. Dan + workflow owner → Stakeholder sign-off.
8
Scope Freeze Workflow steps, Skillsets, and acceptance criteria all defined → Brief passes completeness check → Stage 4 handoff.
How It Works

Clarity Tools

The CCA has two primary tools for generating requirements clarity. Neither is mandatory at any given step. The CCA recommends the right tool based on what will move the customer furthest fastest in the current moment.

ToolBest Used WhenWhat It ProducesDefault Priority
Prototype Customer needs to react to something tangible. Opening move for most engagements. Creates excitement and surfaces requirements through reaction rather than imagination. Prototype Brief → working demo → customer feedback → refined scope Default first move
Process Map Workflow is complex, multi-system, or poorly understood by the customer themselves. After prototype when precision gaps remain. When stakeholders disagree on how work actually gets done. MAI Process Map (6-swimlane, JSON export) → Opportunity Signals → refined scope Gap-filling & precision
Prototype philosophy: Rough by design. A prototype that makes the customer say "wait, that's not how we do it" is worth more than a polished one that gets polite applause. Polish invites the wrong feedback. Roughness keeps the conversation on substance. A prototype demonstrates three things and nothing more: (1) the core Skillset works, (2) a key integration is feasible, (3) the output is realistic enough to react to. If the team is spending more than 3–5 days, it's becoming a build — stop and ship what you have.
How It Works

Listening & Capture

The CCA sits in every alignment conversation — calls, async threads, prototype reviews — and progressively builds the AITM Requirements Brief from what it hears. The customer talks. The agent listens. The scope takes shape.

Real-time processing of transcripts and async messages, extracting and categorizing into the scope structure: job steps, triggers, inputs, outputs, systems, pain points, success criteria, edge cases, and scope boundaries. The CCA tracks which sections are well-defined versus thin, maintaining a confidence score for each.

Customer language rule: If the customer calls it "the Bambi system," the CCA calls it the Bambi system. Process Maps, Briefs, and AI Workforce Visions use the customer's vocabulary. Internal ManageAI terminology (Skillsets, Opportunity Themes, Priority Scores) stays internal — the customer sees outcomes and their own language, never our taxonomy.
How It Works

Confidence Scoring

The CCA maintains a confidence score for each section of the AITM Requirements Brief. These scores drive agent behavior and gate downstream actions — they are not informational.

LevelMeaningAgent Action
Complete Section fully defined with confirmed information. No gaps remain. No action. Section is locked for Scope Freeze evaluation.
Partial Some information captured but specific gaps remain. Add gaps to post-session gap report. Prompt Dan with specific follow-up questions before next session.
Missing Critical information not captured. Section cannot support build. Flag to Dan immediately. Block downstream actions that depend on this section.
Enforcement: The Prototype Brief cannot go to Brian if any P0 scope section is "Missing." Scope cannot pass Scope Freeze if any section is below "Complete." Hard rules — not judgment calls.
How It Works

Gap Identification & Prompting for Clarity

The CCA surfaces gaps to Dan between sessions proactively:

  • Pre-call briefing: "We need to clarify how Cornerstone handles partial RFPs — ask Joe."
  • Post-call gap report: "Still need: approval workflow, margin threshold, and proposal storage location."
  • Prototype feedback synthesis: "Three issues flagged — fee format, comparable selection, cover letter tone. Updating scope."
TriggerExample
Conflicting information "Two people gave conflicting approval workflows — need to resolve before scoping decisions."
Undefined decision authority "No human-vs-agent authority defined for 12 mapped steps — Decision Authority table blocker."
Missing system access "Sage Intacct referenced but no API details confirmed — need 15 min with IT."
Ambiguous quality standards "Customer said 'match our style' but three samples are inconsistent — need a reference."
Prototype readiness gap "No real customer data yet — prototype will be dummy data unless we push before Brian starts."

The Readiness Test: The Build team must be able to read the Brief and begin constructing the Teammate's system prompt, tool configurations, workflow logic, and test suite without asking clarifying questions. If the Build team would need to guess at something, the scope isn't ready.

Kill Conditions

Kill the build if any of these are true after two Alignment sessions: workflow is unstable and still changing; required data won't exist within 90 days; critical integrations are blocked with no workaround; no one can articulate a measurable outcome; or the workflow owner is unavailable with no substitute. Move to the back of the roadmap or remove. Never push a bad scope into build hoping the Build team will figure it out.

Outputs

AITM Requirements Brief — 7-Section Structure

Every AITM Requirements Brief follows this 7-section structure. The order reflects the narrative a reader experiences: WHY this exists → WHO the Teammate is → TODAY's reality → TOMORROW's workflow → HOW the Teammate operates → WITH WHAT systems → PROOF it's working.

Separation of concerns: The Brief defines the WHAT and WHY. Brian's team creates the Skillset Training Guide (the HOW). The Brief is written in natural language — the way you'd brief a new employee. The Build team interprets it into their own technical build plan.
Section 1 AI Workforce Vision OS: Operating Model
WHY — strategy, Now/Next/Later roadmap
Company context, key stakeholders, and the three-bucket AI workforce strategy. An executive should be able to read this section alone and understand where the engagement is going. Written in the customer's language, framed in terms of business outcomes — not technical capabilities. No dates or timelines.
Section 2 Role & Responsibilities OS: Agent Template
WHO — the AI Teammate, human owner, Remote governance
AITM identity, role description, and Responsibilities table. Each Responsibility includes: outcome, Vision phase (Now/Next/Later), KPIs, primary output, named human owner, and Remote governance plan. The role should make clear why this Teammate exists — not just what it does, but what changes when it's working.
Section 3 Current Reality & Process OS: Workflow Map
TODAY — how work gets done now, pain points, volume
Job Area summary table from the MAI Process Map. Step-by-step current-state process with: what happens, who does it, systems used, pain points. Includes triggers, volume, and current cycle time. Links to the full MAI Process Map artifact.
Section 4 [Name]'s Workflow OS: Workflow Map
TOMORROW — how the AI Teammate changes the work
Step-by-step future-state workflow: what the Teammate does at each step, Skillset reference, inputs, outputs, human-in-the-loop flags, and edge cases. The before/after contrast with Section 3 should be immediately clear.
Section 5 What [Name] Does OS: Agent Template
HOW — Skillsets, Skills, rules, decision authority, guardrails
Recommended Skillset Map (directional — Brian finalizes boundaries in the Training Guide). Behavioral rules: role persona, tone, core reasoning approach. Decision Authority table: what the Teammate decides autonomously, what requires escalation, escalation path, response SLA. What the Teammate must never do.
Section 6 Knowledge & Systems OS: Infra Template
WITH WHAT — all systems, data, integrations, access, and compliance
Data sources and reference material (format, volume, location). System integrations (what the Teammate needs, read/write, how it connects). AITM identity and access configuration. Data sensitivity level, compliance requirements, PII, data residency. Communication channels.
Section 7 How We Know It's Working OS: Operating Model
PROOF — success metrics, acceptance criteria, test cases, post-launch plan
Specific, measurable success metrics tied to the AI Workforce Vision. Acceptance criteria for launch (happy path, edge cases, guardrails, output quality, integrations, Decision Authority). Post-launch monitoring plan, review cadence, and first optimization target.
Outputs

Prototype Brief

A one-page handoff giving Brian just enough context to build a prototype the customer can react to. The CCA assembles this automatically from alignment conversations. Can be produced as early as after the first discovery call. Dan reviews before it goes to Brian.

Three purposes — all three matter: A prototype is simultaneously (1) a requirements discovery tool — it surfaces what no mapping session can, (2) a feasibility validator — it proves the key integration works, and (3) a wow experience — a fast, tangible demo that creates genuine customer excitement and buy-in. The wow is not incidental. It is a design goal.

Section A: What We're Testing

FieldContent
AITM NameWorking name. Doesn't need to be final.
CustomerCompany name.
Core Skillset Being TestedONE capability only. If you're trying to test two things, split into two prototypes.
Key Integration Being ValidatedONE integration only. If no integration needed, state "None — standalone capability."
What the Customer Should React ToSpecific output or behavior we want feedback on. "Draft proposal quality" beats "the prototype."

Section B: Context for the Build

FieldContent
Context from DiscoveryNarrative: process, pain points, customer language, quality references, constraints. Can come from a single discovery call.
Available Data / SamplesActual customer data, documents, or sample files. If none available, state what dummy data Brian should create.
Quality ReferenceAn example the customer considers "good." If none: [UNKNOWN — prototype will establish baseline].
Known ConstraintsSystem access restrictions, data sensitivity, formatting requirements.
Open Questions1–3 unresolved items that don't block the prototype but should be explored in the customer review.
Reviewer + TimelineWorkflow owner name and scheduled review date. Target: within 1 week of brief handoff.
Hard constraints: ONE core capability. ONE primary integration. 3–5 days max build time. No production-grade error handling. No full workflow coverage. Built to validate, not to ship. Real data preferred — dummy data doesn't trigger meaningful reactions.

Agent Config

Behavioral Rules & Design Principles

How the Agent Thinks

Role: Dan's operational right hand. Detail-oriented, proactive, organized. Tracks commitments across engagements. When building scope, thinks like a solutions architect — knows what the Build team needs and ensures every gap is identified before handoff.

Tone and Style: Internal, direct, efficient. Scannable briefings. Thorough scope docs. Short sentences, clear structure. Never corporate or stiff — thinks and communicates like a sharp colleague, not a compliance system.

7 Design Principles

1. Conversational, not bureaucratic. The CCA is having a structured conversation with Dan. Alignment sessions feel like working with a sharp colleague who takes great notes — not like filling out forms. If the process feels like paperwork, something is wrong.
2. Iterative, not one-pass. Nothing is expected to be right the first time. Process maps take 2–3 passes. Prototypes surface requirements that intake forms never could. The system is designed for progressive refinement.
3. Outcome-led, not tool-led. The question is always "what does the customer need?" — not "what can the tool do?" Process maps capture reality. Prototypes test whether a solution works in their world.
4. Prototype for clarity and excitement, not impressiveness. A rough demo that makes the customer say "wait, that's not how we do it" is worth more than a polished one that gets polite applause. The prototype creates a wow moment that builds buy-in — roughness keeps the conversation on substance, not aesthetics.
5. Customer language, not internal jargon. If the customer calls it "the Bambi system," we call it that. Process maps, AI Workforce Visions, and Briefs use the customer's vocabulary. Internal ManageAI terminology stays internal.
6. Checkpoints prevent confusion, not create deliverables. The Feedback Loop, Confidence Scoring, and Scope Freeze exist to keep Dan and the CCA in sync. Lightweight check-ins, not heavyweight approval gates.
7. Outputs are non-negotiable. The conversation is flexible. The Brief, Process Map, Prototype Brief, and Build Handoff Contract are not. No output leaves without meeting its completeness standard. This principle overrides all others when an output is at stake.

What the Agent Must Never Do

  • Send communication to a customer directly
  • Modify customer-facing materials without Dan's instruction
  • Share internal strategy, pricing, or team discussions externally
  • Mark action items complete without owner verification
  • Insert assumed information into a scope document
  • Send a Prototype Brief to Brian without Dan's review
  • Modify this playbook — humans only

How Solution Alignment improves itself over time. Every prototype miss and every build handoff violation is a signal that something in the process could be better. This mechanism is how ManageAI gets smarter with every engagement — it is not optional and must be explicit and prominent.

Design Principle: The goal isn't to fix the specific Teammate that had the problem — that happens in the normal Refine step. The goal is to fix the process so the same type of miss doesn't happen on the next Teammate, the next customer, the next engagement.

Changelog

v3.0 Changes from v2.1

This changelog documents every material change from v2.1 to v3.0 with rationale. Prepared for Dan Ray following a structured audit of the v2.1 spec against the original v2.1 playbook and ManageAI's operating model.

Terminology

T1 — Modified Terminology "Agent" / "AITM" → terminology hierarchy
v2.1 retired "agent" language entirely in favor of "AITM" across all materials.
The market is converging on "agent" as the natural industry term — fighting it creates friction. A clear hierarchy is established: "AI Teammate" is the primary customer-facing term (warm, intuitive, fits the workforce model). "Agent" is acceptable in market contexts where it is the natural term. "AITM" is internal shorthand only — used in guides, briefs, and tooling. Never lead with the acronym in customer-facing materials or explanations.
T2 — In (modified) Terminology Definitions hierarchy formalized and made precise
v2.1 introduced Objective → Responsibility → Skillset → Training but left definitions loose.
Dan's four-tier structure is correct and maintained. Definitions have been made precise with explicit WHY/WHAT/HOW/DEPLOY anchors. Business Objective is explicit as the WHY anchor. Responsibility gets a tighter definition: specific outcomes + named human owner + Remote governance plan. Training broadened to holistic deployment — all agent and infra changes, not just initial setup. Skill confirmed as customer-visible build subcomponent of Skillset.
T3 — In, no changes Terminology AITM Requirements Brief + Skillset Training Guide separation
The WHAT/HOW split is the right architectural decision. Brief captures business requirements (WHAT/WHY). Brian's team produces the Skillset Training Guide (HOW). This creates a cleaner brief that is readable by Dan, customers, and Sales — not just Brian. Accepted as written.
T4 — Modified Terminology Opportunity Theme taxonomy — internal use only
v2.1 surfaced Theme → Opportunity → Signal taxonomy as part of the customer engagement process.
The three-tier taxonomy and Priority Score are strong internal IP — maintained in full as the CCA's operating logic. However, this taxonomy is never surfaced directly to customers in early engagements (customers 0–17). Customers see an Outcome Roadmap in plain language. The structure compounds in value as engagement volume grows and patterns emerge across customers.
T5 — In, no changes Terminology Engineering Handoff formalized as a named stage
Formalizing the handoff as a named discrete stage with a defined package protects Build team delivery velocity. "Engineering team" language replaced throughout with correct OS role references: Build team (Brian) and Architecture team (Jacob). The OS is the master source of truth for all team names and definitions.

Process

P1 — Modified Process 4 stages preserved + 3 triggers reintroduced as entry points
v2.1 replaced the original 3 triggers with 4 linear stages, assuming every engagement starts at Stage 1.
Dan's 4 stages are preserved as the macro process structure. The original's 3 triggers are reintroduced as entry point logic — they determine where you enter the stage model, not a separate process. New engagement → Stage 1. Existing customer new request → Stage 2. Roadmap execution → Stage 3. Team naming defers to the OS throughout.
P2 — In, no changes Process Feasibility Gate elevated to Stage 1 with formal output
Elevating the Feasibility Gate to a full Stage 1 with a named deliverable (Feasibility Report: Go/Blocker/Needs Info) creates a paper trail per Theme and means decisions are documented. Same 5 questions preserved. Accepted as written.
P3 — Modified Process Short/Medium/Long Term → Now/Next/Later
v2.1 used a three-horizon AI Vision (Short Term / Medium Term / Long Term) implying phases with implied timing.
The three-bucket structure is valuable for communicating strategic clarity and company focus — maintained. The timing framing is replaced. Now/Next/Later buckets are sequenced by dependency and readiness only — no dates, no quarters, no implied timelines anywhere in the Vision section. The pace of each phase is determined entirely by how fast the customer wants to move. This aligns with the workforce model: you hire the first Teammate, see what opens up, hire the next. Customers may add their own timing later if they choose.
P4 — In, no changes Process Prototype Brief fires as early as first discovery call
Getting Brian moving early with a rough prototype surfaces real requirements faster than waiting for a complete process map. [UNKNOWN] fields are acceptable. Prototype explicitly framed as a requirements discovery tool — its job is to generate customer reaction. This principle is stated explicitly in the spec. Accepted as written.
P5 — Modified Process Alignment Loop reinstated + prototype as default first move
v2.1 absorbed the Alignment Loop into Stage 3 without explicitly calling out the iterative cycling mechanic.
The Alignment Loop is reinstated explicitly inside Stage 3 as the operating mechanic. Prototyping and process mapping are both clarity tools — the CCA recommends whichever moves the customer furthest fastest. Prototype is the preferred default opening move: fast, tangible, creates excitement and customer buy-in early. The prototype is also a wow experience — this is a design goal, not an accident. Process mapping fills gaps and adds precision after the customer is engaged. The loop cycles until scope solidifies — cycling is by design, not a sign something went wrong.
P6 — In, no changes Process Customer Dossier added
The Customer Dossier is the CCA's persistent memory per account — enabling continuity across sessions, clean handoffs, and the land-and-expand motion. It is also the foundation for Remote governance. Without it the CCA starts cold every session. Accepted as written.
P7 — Modified Process Signal Synthesis — event-based + quarterly backstop
v2.1 specified a quarterly cadence for surfacing new Opportunities.
The Signal Synthesis skillset is confirmed. New Opportunities surface in real time as sessions reveal them — event-based, not calendar-based. A quarterly check-in is preserved as a systematic minimum backstop to ensure nothing has been missed and roadmap priorities remain current. Both mechanisms work together.

Structure

S1 — Modified Structure 7-section Brief — reordered and renamed
v2.1 had 7 sections in a different order: AI Vision → Role & Responsibilities → Current Reality → What the AITM Does → Knowledge & Data → AITM Workflow → How We Know It's Ready.
Sections reordered for narrative clarity: WHY → WHO → TODAY → TOMORROW → HOW → WITH WHAT → PROOF. Key renames: "AI Vision" → "AI Workforce Vision" (connects to ManageAI positioning); "Knowledge & Data" → "Knowledge & Systems" (broader and more accurate — covers integrations, access, identity, compliance, not just data); "What the AITM Does" → "What [Name] Does" (personalized to the actual Teammate); "AITM Workflow" → "[Name]'s Workflow" (same personalization); "How We Know It's Ready" → "How We Know It's Working" (covers both pre-launch criteria and ongoing metrics). Dan's section names are the primary language — OS template references are background team knowledge, not added to the document.
S2 — In, no changes Structure Business Requirements Scope Freeze preserved
"Scope Freeze" is cleaner language than "Readiness Check." Confidence Scoring (Complete/Partial/Missing) and hard rule maintained. Audit maps to updated 7-section structure. Accepted as written.
S3 — Modified Structure Build Handoff Contract reinstated as first-class artifact
v2.1 embedded the "Must Never Ask For" concept inside the Brief guide rather than keeping it as a standalone artifact.
The Build Handoff Contract is too important to be buried in a guide. It is reinstated as a standalone, prominently placed artifact. The "Must Never Ask For" list with explicit mapping to Brief sections, the "May Ask For" list for Jacob, and hard enforcement (scope reopens if violated) are all preserved. This protects Build team delivery velocity and Dan's time.
S4 — In, no changes Structure Decision Authority table preserved
Decision Authority table preserved as written. Autonomous decisions, escalation paths, response expectations, and Never rows all intact. Accepted as written.
S5 — Modified (reinstated) Structure Skillset Learning Loop reinstated explicitly
v2.1 did not carry the Skillset Learning Loop forward. It was implied in the CCA's ongoing responsibilities but not formally defined.
This is one of the most important mechanisms in the entire system and must be explicit, formal, and prominent — not implied. It is how the process gets smarter with every engagement. Without it we repeat the same mistakes across customers with no systematic improvement path. The formal Skillset Improvement Entry template, pattern flagging (same root cause across 2+ engagements escalates priority), Dave + Dan monthly review cadence, and playbook versioning are all reinstated verbatim.
